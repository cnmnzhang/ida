# Direction Update: IDA Research Explorer
**Date:** March 5, 2026
**Reference:** `docs/proposal20260215.md`

---

## What This Document Is

The February 15 proposal described a focused informatics project: build a visualization that contrasts population reference intervals (popRI) with personalized reference intervals (perRI) for hemoglobin, use ferritin as a confirmatory biomarker, and surface clinical underdiagnosis of IDA from UWMC EHR data. Three weeks later, the direction has shifted in ways worth documenting. The shift is not a retreat from the original goal — it is a sharpening of it, driven by two sources of friction: what public tools actually produce when asked to simulate IDA, and what the global epidemiological record reveals about why IDA burden is where it is.

---

## 1. What We Learned From Public Data Tools

### 1.1 Synthea forces you to confront the phenotyping problem

The original proposal treated IDA as a well-defined phenotype: a patient has IDA if they have low ferritin, low hemoglobin, and a confirmatory diagnosis code. Running Synthea at scale — 5,000 patients, Washington state, female 18–65 — revealed immediately that this phenotype is almost invisible in structured data, even in a simulation built to produce it.

At n=5,000, the IDA-specific SNOMED codes (87522002, 234347009) never fired. The only anemia code generated was the generic *Anemia (disorder)* (271737000), which appeared in 36.4% of the filtered cohort. This is not a Synthea quirk to be corrected — it is an accurate reflection of how anemia is coded in practice. IDA-specific coding requires a physician to order confirmatory labs, interpret them, and document a specific etiology. All of those steps have failure points. Synthea's module fires the generic code because that is what most EHR systems contain.

This shifted a core assumption of the project. The February proposal treated diagnosis codes as a starting point for identification. What Synthea showed is that the diagnosis code should be treated as an *outcome variable* of the detection pipeline, not its input. If a patient appears in conditions.csv with anemia coded, some detection process already worked. The interesting population is the one that does not appear there — and finding them requires something other than code lookups.

### 1.2 Ferritin is not the confirmation pathway we assumed

The February proposal was explicit: FER < 30 ng/mL defines a positive iron deficiency screen. The UWMC protocol even described ordering ferritin on excess specimens within 24 hours of identifying a flagged patient. Synthea broke this assumption in a different way: it generates ferritin, but floors it at 30.1 µg/L. Every ferritin observation in the synthetic cohort is normal. No patient crosses the threshold.

This is Synthea modeling healthy physiology by default. Iron studies are only generated when the anemia module fires, and when it fires, the iron stores are still described in the normal range (a plausible but simplified model of anemia stages). The result is that ferritin carries zero discriminative signal in synthetic data.

The instructive thing here is not that Synthea is wrong — it is that the ferritin ordering assumption is more fragile than originally stated. In real EHR data, ferritin is ordered sporadically and incompletely, predominantly in patients who are already suspected of having anemia. If we condition our detection pipeline on ferritin availability, we exclude exactly the patients who were never suspected. That is the population the perRI approach is designed to find.

The practical consequence: the pipeline shifted to HB-only signals. The Bayesian adaptive setpoint — the personalized running estimate of each patient's expected HB, updated with every measurement — became the primary detection mechanism. It does not require ferritin to be ordered. It does not require a diagnosis code to exist. It requires only longitudinal HB data, which is the most consistently collected laboratory value in routine care.

### 1.3 The "diagnostic gap" is structurally different from what we expected

The February proposal characterized the diagnostic gap as the central public health informatics problem: patients with lab-defined anemia who have no anemia diagnosis on record. In the synthetic n=5,000 female cohort, this gap is almost closed: only 1 of 1,674 patients has lab anemia without a coded diagnosis. The gap is not 3–4% as we estimated; it is 0.1%.

But the opposite cell — coded anemia with HB now normal — contains 609 patients (36.4%). Diagnoses persist after lab recovery. This is the mismatch that Synthea reproduces well because it comes from how conditions.csv works: diagnoses accumulate and are not automatically retracted when the underlying measurement normalizes.

The real diagnostic gap in clinical practice likely looks quite different. Code-based surveillance misses patients who are never brought to the attention of the clinical system at all — patients who are iron-depleted, whose HB is declining within the "normal" range, who attend clinic infrequently and whose lab values are never flagged because they have not yet crossed a threshold. Synthea cannot generate this population because every patient in Synthea has HB measured and the anemia module fires deterministically at the threshold. The subtle pre-threshold decline — the case perRI is specifically designed to detect — does not appear in Synthea's output.

This means synthetic data is not the right validation environment for the core clinical hypothesis. It is useful for building and stress-testing the detection pipeline (the code, the algorithm, the interface), but the question of whether Bayesian setpoint drops capture real IDA before diagnosis requires real EHR data. Synthea confirmed the pipeline works; it cannot confirm the hypothesis.

### 1.4 The adaptive setpoint is now the methodological core

The original proposal mentioned perRI as something derived from "ongoing research." It has now become the computational foundation of the project. The Bayesian conjugate Gaussian model — which tracks each patient's expected HB as a personalized posterior that updates with every measurement — does several things that the February framing did not anticipate:

- It produces a detection signal that is self-referencing: a patient is flagged because *their* trajectory has deviated from *their* own baseline, not because they crossed a universal threshold.
- It produces a uncertainty estimate (sigma) alongside every prediction, enabling the threshold to be expressed in either absolute g/dL terms or normalized z-score units. A 1.5 g/dL drop for a high-variance patient is less alarming than the same drop for someone whose HB has been steady for years.
- It degrades gracefully: patients with only one HB reading receive no setpoint; patients with two or more readings begin accumulating a personalized estimate that narrows with each new observation.
- It does not depend on any code, any diagnosis, or any ordering decision. It runs directly on the lab measurement stream.

The tunable threshold slider in the Policy Builder is the user-facing expression of this: a single parameter that lets a surveillance analyst ask "if I flag everyone whose HB has dropped X g/dL from their personal baseline, how many patients does that identify, and who are they?" The investigation panel — patient-level HB chart with adaptive mu trajectory and sigma bands — is the tool for auditing individual cases.

## 2. What We Learned From the Global and Temporal Perspective

### 2.1 The 2009 inflection point is real and underexplained

The global trends visualization in the IDA Research Explorer (page 1) documents a pattern that appears consistently across GBD data: the rate of improvement in IDA mortality and DALY burden decelerated sharply around 2009 and has remained sluggish since. Page 3 — "Post-2009 Stagnation" — is named for this pattern. It is not a small fluctuation.

The 2008–2009 global financial crisis is the most plausible candidate explanation, and the mechanism is not subtle. IDA is a nutritional condition. Its primary drivers are dietary iron intake, absorption, and loss. All three are sensitive to the kinds of disruptions that economic crises produce: food insecurity increases when purchasing power falls; fortified foods are replaced by cheaper staples; micronutrient supplementation programs are among the first line items cut when government budgets contract; clinical encounters that would have identified iron depletion are deferred when healthcare is unaffordable.

The populations most affected by IDA — women of reproductive age in low- and middle-income countries — are also the populations most exposed to the downstream effects of economic contraction. The coincidence of the GBD deceleration with the financial crisis, in the right direction and at roughly the right magnitude, is not proof of causation, but it is a strong prior.

### 2.2 This changes how we should interpret the US burden

The US-focused visualization (page 2) shows a different pattern: IDA prevalence in the US has been relatively flat over the same period. The US did not experience the same food insecurity shock as lower-income countries, but the 2009 recession did change healthcare utilization patterns in ways that are relevant to IDA surveillance.

Following the crisis, screening rates fell in lower-income populations, particularly among uninsured and underinsured women who delayed or skipped routine care. If IDA is identified primarily through routine lab work ordered during clinical encounters, and if those encounters became less frequent in the populations at highest risk, then flat prevalence in US data may partly reflect reduced detection rather than stable or improving disease burden. The signal we observe is always a function of both the disease and the healthcare contact that surfaces it.

This is the measurement problem that popRI-based surveillance is ill-suited to address and that perRI-based surveillance is specifically designed for: a patient who attends clinic infrequently but whose HB is systematically declining will eventually cross a popRI threshold and be flagged — but only at the moment they happen to appear. The perRI approach, applied to whatever HB data does exist, surfaces the trajectory before the threshold is crossed, and does so with the data that is already present in the record.

### 2.3 The temporal framing changes what "underdiagnosis" means

The February proposal used "underdiagnosis" in the clinical sense: a patient who has IDA but no IDA diagnosis on record. This is a cross-sectional definition. The global and temporal perspective suggests a second definition that is more relevant to public health: a *period* during which a population's burden is greater than surveillance systems can detect, because the social and economic conditions that drive access to diagnosis have deteriorated.

The post-2009 stagnation may not mean that progress on IDA prevention has stopped. It may mean that progress has been partially offset by a reduced capacity to identify and treat cases — a surveillance deficit that looks, in aggregate data, like a disease plateau. If that is true, the burden estimates from GBD and similar sources during this period are lower bounds. True burden, adjusted for detection probability, is higher.

This is a hypothesis the project cannot test with Synthea or even with a single EHR dataset. But it should shape how we frame the contribution of the perRI tool. It is not only a clinical screening artifact. It is an attempt to shift the detection probability upward — to identify more of the true cases that exist in a patient population — and by doing so, produce a more accurate picture of burden than code-based or threshold-based surveillance alone can provide.

### 2.4 The global framing justifies the multi-scale design

The IDA Research Explorer was organized into four levels: global trends, US burden, post-2009 stagnation, and a patient-level policy builder. This felt like a natural progression at the time, but the global and temporal analysis retrospectively justifies the structure as something more principled.

Population-level surveillance produces the estimates that make IDA visible as a public health priority. Those estimates are shaped by macro-economic forces (the 2009 crisis) that are not legible in any individual patient's record. Individual patient records contain the physiological signals (HB trajectories) that population estimates aggregate and therefore flatten. The perRI approach connects those two scales: it is a patient-level computation whose aggregate output — how many patients are being flagged, and at what rate — constitutes a form of surveillance that is more sensitive to early-stage disease than either popRI thresholds or diagnosis codes.

The Policy Builder is where those scales meet. A surveillance analyst looking at the flagged count for a given threshold and trigger is looking at a synthetic population-level estimate produced by running a patient-level algorithm across 1,674 individuals. The investigation panel then lets them descend back to the patient level to ask: is this flagged patient's decline consistent with what I know about IDA? The multi-scale design is not cosmetic. It reflects the structure of the problem.

## 3. Where the Work Stands on March 5, 2026

### What has been built

- A synthetic cohort pipeline (Synthea, Washington, n=5,000, seed=1) and a filtered cohort of 1,674 female patients aged 18–65.
- A Bayesian adaptive setpoint model with per-patient HB trajectories, uncertainty estimates, and z-score normalized drop signals.
- A Surveillance Policy Builder with four trigger modes (lab-defined anemia, coded anemia, diagnostic gap, Bayesian setpoint drop), a tunable threshold, and a patient investigation panel showing adaptive mu trajectories with sigma bands.
- A data quality report documenting Synthea's structural limitations for IDA research.

### What has not changed from February 15

The core informatics claim has not changed: the perRI approach identifies physiological deviation that popRI-based surveillance misses. The claim is now supported by a working implementation in synthetic data and a clearer account of why real EHR data is required to validate the clinical hypothesis.

The population scope has not changed: women 18–65 in routine outpatient care remains the target.

The stakeholder framing has not changed: public health agencies, epidemiologists, and population health teams remain the primary intended users.

### What has changed

Ferritin has moved from a confirmatory biomarker central to the detection pipeline to a secondary signal of interest — present in real data, relevant to understanding iron store depletion, but not a feasible gating condition for case identification. The detection logic now runs on HB alone.

Diagnosis codes have moved from a starting point for identification to an outcome variable. We are building a system to find patients who do not have codes, not to refine identification among those who do.

The IDA phenotype has been replaced, for the purpose of this pipeline, with the concept of a *setpoint departure* — a within-person signal that does not require any etiology to be confirmed. The pipeline flags; a clinician (or a ferritin result) confirms. This is a more honest representation of what an informatics system can contribute.

The temporal and global context has been incorporated into the research framing. The post-2009 stagnation is not a background fact; it is part of the argument for why better surveillance tools matter.
