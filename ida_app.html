Global Epidemiology · 1990–2021

Iron Deficiency Anemia
Worldwide Trends

Global anemia prevalence fell from 28.2% in 1990 to 24.3% in 2021 — 1.92 billion cases — with iron deficiency remaining the dominant cause at 66.2% of all anemia burden. Progress has been deeply uneven: adult males improved dramatically while women of reproductive age and children under five saw near-stagnation.

In 2021, 31.2% of women had anemia globally versus 17.5% of men. The divergence is most extreme during reproductive years: among adults aged 15–49, anemia prevalence in women reached 33.7% versus just 11.3% in men. The regions with the lowest anemia burden are Australasia (5.7%), Western Europe (6.0%), and North America (6.8%), while Western sub-Saharan Africa (47.4%), South Asia (35.7%), and Central sub-Saharan Africa (35.7%) face the greatest burden.

United States · GBD 2019 + NHANES 2021–2023

IDA Burden in the
United States

Anemia-related DALYs in the US increased 26% from 332,449 in 1990 to 418,855 in 2019 — a troubling rise in a wealthy nation with food fortification programs. Female individuals account for nearly three-quarters of all US anemia DALYs, with dietary iron deficiency contributing more than half of total burden.

📊 What is a DALY?

A Disability-Adjusted Life Year (DALY) = 1 year of healthy life lost, either to premature death or to living with illness/disability. For IDA in the US, DALYs are almost entirely YLDs (years lived with disability) since anemia rarely causes direct death here. 418,855 DALYs in 2019 means roughly 419,000 full years of healthy life were erased that year — lost to fatigue, cognitive impairment, and reduced work capacity. Predominantly in women.

Overall US anemia prevalence (2021–23)

US anemia prevalence is 13.0% in females versus 5.5% in males overall (NHANES 2021–2023). Female adolescents ages 12–19 have the highest prevalence at 17.4% — reflecting the iron drain of menstruation onset with diets that rarely compensate. Black non-Hispanic women face an anemia prevalence of 31.4% — more than double the rate of non-Hispanic White women — the highest burden of any demographic group in the US.

Causal Analysis · NHANES + GBD 1999–2021

Why Progress Stopped
Around 2009

Three converging structural forces erased the gains made during the 1990s and early 2000s. None is a dramatic singular event — together they created a slow-moving, population-wide trap that standard interventions like iron fortification couldn't overcome.

−9.5%

🥩 Dietary Iron Intake Collapsed

Female adult dietary iron intake fell ~9.5% between 1999–2018. There was a 15.3% reduction in beef (the richest heme-iron source) and a 21.5% increase in chicken. Of US food items re-analyzed in 2015, 62.4% had lower iron concentrations than in 1999.

Hepcidin ↑

⚖️ Obesity Blocks Absorption

Hepcidin levels are elevated in obese individuals due to subclinical inflammation, reducing iron absorption and blunting the effects of iron fortification — so low iron status in overweight individuals results from combined nutritional and functional iron deficiency.

57% kcal

🍟 Ultra-Processed Food Rise

Ultra-processed foods rose from 53.5% to 57% of US adult caloric intake between 2001–2018, while minimally processed foods fell from 32.7% to 27.4% — a consistent trend across all demographic subgroups except Hispanics.

The Hepcidin Loop — Why Obesity Blocks Iron Even When You Eat Enough

🔥

Fat tissue secretes IL-6

Visceral fat cells produce interleukin-6 and pro-inflammatory cytokines, creating chronic low-grade inflammation even in otherwise "healthy" obese individuals.

🛡️

IL-6 triggers hepcidin synthesis

The liver responds to IL-6 by producing hepcidin. Normally this prevents iron overload — but in obesity, hepcidin stays chronically elevated even when the body is iron-deficient.

🔒

Hepcidin locks ferroportin

Hepcidin binds to ferroportin — the only known iron export channel on gut cells, macrophages, and hepatocytes — causing its degradation. Iron gets trapped inside cells and can't enter circulation.

🩸

Result: functional iron deficiency

Blood tests may show normal ferritin (stored iron looks fine), but serum iron is low. In overweight and obese women, the enhancement of iron absorption by ascorbic acid is only half that seen in normal-weight women. Standard supplements often fail. Weight loss reverses this.

The ~2009 inflection point coincides with three forces peaking simultaneously: US adult female obesity crossed 36%, beef consumption was in sustained secular decline, and the 2008 financial crisis reduced food access for lower-income households. Men's iron requirement is 8 mg/day; women of reproductive age need 18 mg/day — 2.25× more — to offset menstrual losses. A 9.5% drop in dietary iron intake is barely noticeable for men; for women already near the threshold, it tips the balance into deficiency.

Why the Same Trends Hit Women Far Harder Than Men
Daily iron requirements vs. actual intakes — the margin for error
Men have always been eating well above their 8 mg/day requirement — a 9.5% intake decline still leaves a comfortable buffer. Women's 18 mg/day requirement means they were already running close to the margin; the same decline pushed millions into deficiency. Dietary iron recommendations are 18 mg/day for women of reproductive age compared to 8 mg/day for men, rising to 27 mg/day during pregnancy.
