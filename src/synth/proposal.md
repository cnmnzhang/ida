# Topic
Developing a patient-level visualization to reduce underdiagnosis of iron deficiency anemia by contrasting population reference intervals with personalized physiological reference intervals derived from longitudinal hemoglobin data.
# Public Health Problem
Iron deficiency anemia (IDA) is one of the most common nutritional deficiencies worldwide and disproportionately affects adult women in the United States. IDA is associated with fatigue, impaired cognition, reduced work capacity, adverse pregnancy outcomes, and diminished quality of life. Despite its prevalence and clinical impact, IDA frequently goes undetected or is diagnosed late in routine care.
From a public health perspective, surveillance and detection of IDA rely heavily on diagnosis codes and hemoglobin (HB) thresholds derived from population reference intervals (popRI). These approaches identify disease only after substantial physiological depletion has occurred and fail to capture prolonged pre-diagnostic phases during which intervention may be more effective. As a result, population burden is systematically underestimated, opportunities for prevention are missed, and observed incidence is biased toward later stages of disease. 
# Public Health Informatics Problem
Although longitudinal laboratory data are routinely collected in electronic health records (EHRs), existing informatics workflows remain anchored to popRI interpretation and univariate thresholds. Clinically meaningful within-person declines in HB may remain “normal” by population standards and therefore fail to trigger further evaluation.
Ferritin (FER) testing, which is central to confirming IDA, is typically ordered only after anemia or symptoms are suspected. However, patients whose physiology is changing in a manner consistent with early iron depletion but who have not yet crossed population thresholds remain invisible to both clinical and public health monitoring. The informatics challenge is therefore one of representation that makes early physiological deviation visible when standard threshold-based workflows fail to do so.
# Disease Context
This project focuses on iron deficiency anemia, using HB as the primary longitudinal physiological signal and FER as a confirmatory marker of iron deficiency evaluation. HB reflects anemia severity may remain within population-defined normal ranges during early iron depletion, while FER reflects iron stores and provides evidence of whether iron deficiency has been clinically assessed. In this project, FER values below 30 ng/mL are used to define a positive iron deficiency screen. Together, these measures provide a pragmatic and interpretable framework for examining IDA underdiagnosis.
# Scope
This project uses de-identified outpatient EHR data from UWMC, on women aged 18–65 receiving routine clinical care. Individuals with current diagnoses known to confound FER interpretation are excluded. 
# Stakeholders
Key stakeholders include public health agencies for whom improved visibility into under-detected iron deficiency could inform monitoring and prevention strategies. Epidemiologists studying chronic disease burden and health inequities may use the tool to examine bias introduced by diagnosis timing and threshold-based detection. Health systems and population health teams could evaluate secondary use of EHR data to improve targeted case-finding and follow-up workflows. 
Data Sources and Data Types
The project uses de-identified longitudinal EHR data, including HB measurements with timestamps, FER results when obtained, and diagnosis codes mapped to PheCodes to support interoperable phenotype definitions and therefore standardized inclusion criteria. Demographics, of age and sex, are used for inclusion criteria.
# Informatics Task
The primary informatics task is to develop an underdiagnosis pipeline to identify individuals who would likely remain undetected under population-threshold-based interpretation through personalized reference intervals (perRI) developed in ongoing research. Following identification, we will order FER tests from the excess specimens within 24 hours, and FER values below a tunable threshold will identify positive cases. 
A core informatics task is to build a visualization that contrasts popRI with perRI interpretation for HB. The visual will support both patient-level interpretation and surveillance audit.

# Public Health Informatics Contribution
This project demonstrates how a visualization grounded in perRI can complement population-based laboratory interpretation to reduce underdiagnosis of iron deficiency anemia and support earlier, more equitable screening. By integrating HB tests with perRI, targeted surveillance, and interoperable phenotypes, the tool surfaces early physiological change that is invisible to diagnosis-based surveillance and threshold-based screening practices.
Rather than proposing a new diagnostic test, the visualization functions as a screening support artifact. We illustrate how public health informatics tools can enhance screening sensitivity, reduce reliance on late-stage diagnostic signals, and improve population health measurement using routinely collected EHR data.
